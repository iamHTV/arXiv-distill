# [2506.17339] AI is the Strategy: From Agentic AI to Autonomous Business Models

## Problem
Most firms still operate within human-driven or AI-augmented models. But we are entering a phase where agentic AI can increasingly execute the core mechanisms of value creation, delivery, and capture. Prior frameworks treat AI as a tool to support strategy — but this shift reframes AI as THE strategy itself.

## Contribution
1. **Autonomous Business Models (ABMs)**: Distinct managerial and strategic logic in the age of agentic AI. AI executes core value creation, delivery, capture — not just support.
2. **Synthetic competition**: Strategic interactions occur at machine-level speed and scale. New form of competition between AI-led firms.
3. **Cases**: Israeli startup (autonomy by design) + hypothetical Ryanair reconfiguration (AI-driven incumbent).

## Method (core summary)
Conceptual framework: ABMs as distinct logic. Evolution: augmented → autonomous business models. Shows how ABMs reshape competitive advantage through agentic execution, continuous adaptation, gradual offloading of human decision-making. Illustrative cases: Israeli startup pursuing autonomy by design, hypothetical Ryanair as AI-driven incumbent. Discusses implications for strategy, organizational design, governance.

## Actionable Insights
1. **AI as strategy, not tool**: When adopting AI, don't just augment existing processes. Rethink entire business model around AI capabilities. Example: traditional retailer → don't just add AI chatbot. Rethink: AI manages inventory, pricing, customer relationships autonomously.

2. **Synthetic competition preparation**: Competition will shift to machine-level speed. Firms need AI-native competitive advantages. Example: when competitors deploy AI agents negotiating prices real-time, manual pricing strategies become obsolete.

3. **Offload decision-making gradually**: ABMs gradually offload human decisions to AI. Start with low-stakes, expand. Example: start with AI managing routine inventory decisions, expand to pricing, then supplier negotiations.
